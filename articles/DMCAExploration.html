Exploration of the Digital Millennium Copyright Act

Written by: Sophia Hanna
Written for: CSCI 409
Published: 5/7/26

Introduction

The Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) protects copyright owners by regulating the unauthorized sharing of digital files. This law impacts video games, books, movies, music, and any other file one could imagine.

Background and Purpose

The DMCA was introduced by the United States’ Congress on July 29th, 1997 and was passed into law on October 28th, 1998. It was introduced to address increasing copyright infringement opportunities due to the popularization of personal computers.

In the 1990s we saw the rise of peer-to-peer (henceforth referred to as P2P) networks and CD burners. P2P networks function differently from the world wide web most people enjoy today. Most engage in internet activities through a client-server model. A user’s PC (the client) sends and requests information to and from a large server. Even if users send files to each other, it goes through the server first. In a P2P model, users send and request information to and from each other, without the need for a central server. CD burners allow users to read and copy the files on a CD.

These technologies created the perfect storm in 1994, when a PolyGram CD manufacturing plant employee, Bennie Lydell Glover started stealing CDs from the factory and creating his own copies to sell. In 1997, he ramped up his efforts and started selling CDs of albums he had downloaded from P2P chatrooms.

This brings us to the creation of the DMCA. Stories like Glover’s of unauthorized file sharing and copying were becoming increasingly common in the late 1990s. This was a problem for copyright holders and the government had to step in.

Main Parts of the Act

The DMCA is divided into five titles. Title I: WIPO (World Intellectual Property Organization) Treaties Implementation; Title II: Online Copyright Infringement Liability Limitation; Title III: Computer Maintenance or Repair Copyright Exemption; Title IV: Miscellaneous Provisions; and Title V: Protection of Certain Original Designs.

Title I implements policies set forth by the WIPO treaties. This section of the DMCA brings the United States’ copyright law up to global standards. This section also paves the way for international copyright law cooperation between “member states” or other countries in the treaty. Included under the WIPO are protections against altering computer programs to circumvent protection measures. “No person shall circumvent a technological measure that effectively controls access to a work protected under this title.” Today this is known as Digital Rights Management (DRM) technology.

Title II defines limitations on liability for copyright infringement. Specifically, it protects internet service providers (ISPs) from being held liable (with some exceptions) for their customers’ copyright infringement actions. It also defines a process for ISPs to block certain traffic due to copyright infringement concerns via a court order.

Title III is the smallest of the titles. It defines exemptions for creating copies of software for “maintenance or repair” of machines. A modern-day example would be creating an additional Windows installation disk (or USB flash storage drive) for the purpose of repairing a broken or corrupt operating system.

Title IV sets forth miscellaneous policies for specific users and use-cases. For example, it defines exemptions for libraries and content archives. It also further defines regulations around different types of media like audio recordings and “motion pictures” or videos.

The last title, Title V, defines protections of original designs. It defines policy around what is and is not protected, exclusive rights, infringement, fees, copies, transfer of ownership, and many more topics. This title specifically mentions the hulls of aquatic vessels and can also be referred to as the “Vessel Hull Design Protection Act.” This created new protections for vessel hulls that did not exist previously.

Oversight, Enforcement, and Compliance

The DMCA has a chain of enforcement between copyright holders, online service providers, and internet users. Copyright holders will send notices to online service providers regarding specific users and content. This especially applies to websites hosting user-generated content like a video sharing platform or social media website. It is then the responsibility of the online service provider to remove the violating content. Each online service provider can then penalize the offending user according to their own policies.

In the case of Google’s popular video sharing website YouTube, they have a “three strikes and you are out” system. When a copyright holder requests that a video is removed, the user who uploaded that video is issued a “copyright strike.” If a user receives three “strikes” within ninety days before going through YouTube’s “Copyright School” in an attempt to remove “strikes,” their account will be terminated.

Copyright holders can also send requests to ISPs regarding certain users. The ISPs in turn, send cease-and-desist notices and may take other actions in accordance with their own policies. In the case of Comcast’s Xfinity, a large nationwide ISP, the company has the right to take various actions against a user after repeated copyright violation notices. On the Xfinity support website, the company lists actions they may take against DMCA offenders: “Triggering steps under this policy may result in the following: a form of communication that requires you to log in to your account or call us; a temporary suspension of, or other interim measures applied to, your service; or the termination of your Xfinity Internet service.”

Who Must Comply

As previously stated, online service providers (particularly those hosting user-generated content) and ISPs must comply with requests from copyright holders. From there, it is a user’s responsibility to comply with cease-and-desist orders from ISPs and face consequences laid out by the different online service providers.

Penalties and Sanctions

Penalties for violating the DMCA can include fines and the possibility of jail time for severe offences. An official summary of the DMCA from the U.S. Copyright Office states “penalties range up to a $500,000 fine or up to five years imprisonment for a first offense, and up to a $1,000,000 fine or up to 10 years imprisonment for subsequent offenses.” As mentioned previously, and further highlighted by this official summary, libraries, archives, and educational institutions cannot be held criminally liable.

Case Studies

Internet Archive

To illustrate the impact of the DMCA, two case studies will be discussed. First, the Internet Archive, a non-profit archival project, allows users to submit copies of various digital files for archival purposes. This includes books, movies, music, video games, and even entire websites through their “Wayback Machine” project. In 2006, the Internet Archive petitioned for an exemption to the DMCA regarding older computer software.

Later that year, the Librarian of Congress, as recommended by the Register of Copyrights, declared six categories of works will not be subject to the DMCA for the next three years. This included “computer programs and video games distributed in formats that have become obsolete and that require the original media or hardware as a condition of access, when circumvention is accomplished for the purpose of preservation or archival reproduction of published digital works by a library or archive.” This allows the Internet Archive and similar archival projects and libraries to circumvent DRM technologies within computer programs for the purposes of archival.

Today, certain computer software is still exempt from the DMCA. On the Internet Archive, you can find countless files for old video games and other computer software. For example, you can find the files for Full Tilt Pinball: 3D Pinball Space Cadet. Specifically, the demo of the game that was included in Windows 95, Windows 98, Windows 2000, Windows ME, and Windows XP operating systems. Because those operating systems are out of date and obsolete, the game files are allowed to be altered for newer systems and archived.

This case study shows the need for revisions of copyright law as our world changes. As new content mediums arise, old mediums become obsolete. In an effort to preserve media for future generations, copyright law may need to be updated again in the future. This is why every three years, as seen in 2006, copyright law is revisited and new exceptions are added.

Lenz vs. Universal Music Corp.

Another example regarding the DMCA can be seen in the case of Lenz vs. Universal Music Corp. In 2009, Stephanie Lenz made a video of her young kids dancing in her kitchen. In the background of the video, the song “Let’s Go Crazy” by Prince can be heard for approximately twenty seconds. Lenz then uploaded the video to Google’s video sharing website YouTube. Universal Music Corp. issued a DMCA takedown of the video due to it containing copyrighted material. YouTube then issued a copyright “strike” to Lenz’s account and removed the video. Lenz was able to send a counter notification and YouTube reinstated the video.

Lenz filed a lawsuit against Universal Music Corp. in the Northern District of Columbia, claiming that they had misused the DMCA in relation to fair use. Fair use is defined in section 107 of the Copyright Act of 1976. Fair use allows the usage of copyrighted content as long as it is transformative. Some examples listed within the act are “criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching (including multiple copies for classroom use), scholarship, or research.”

The lawsuit moved through the courts, reaching the Ninth Circuit. The Ninth Circuit affirmed that Universal Music Corp. had issued their takedown in bad faith and misused the DMCA. Furthermore, Universal’s copyright screening practices were found to be inadequate and did not take fair use into consideration.

This case not only paved the way for fair use in online content, but also shows that the DMCA, or any other law, does not exist in a vacuum. Copyright law does not change based on the medium. While the DMCA provides specific guidance for digital media, it coexists with other copyright laws and statutes.

Conclusion

While I believe the DMCA is an important law, I can also recognize its flaws. As proven in the case of Lenz vs. Universal Music Corp., the act is prone to misuse either maliciously or accidentally. Another flaw I have witnessed in my personal life is in relation to the enforcement of the law.

Enforcement of the DMCA is largely left up to online service providers and ISPs. When talking with a friend (who shall go unnamed) about this project, she told me that when she was a kid she would download movies and games off of various copyright-infringing websites. Because of this, her ISP (which shall go unnamed) sent multiple cease-and-desist notices to her home. She likely never saw them or understood the potential consequences. This led the ISP to reduce the speed of her household’s internet service for a year, despite her family paying for faster speeds. In this case, the entire household was punished for an individual’s violation of the act.

I cannot think of another law that exists like this, where an entire household can be punished for the actions of a child. While it is true that ISPs reserve the right to take such actions, as previously mentioned in the case of Xfinity, it does not seem like a fair method of law enforcement.

The Digital Millennium Copyright Act, created to address copyright infringement opportunities on the internet, is an overall complex law. It provides protections for copyright holders while allowing important exemptions for educational use, libraries, and archives. However, the act has major flaws regarding misuse and enforcement. I hope to see meaningful updates to the DMCA in my lifetime.
